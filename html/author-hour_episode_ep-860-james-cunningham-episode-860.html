📚 Books by James Cunningham

Savvy Estate Planning

What You Need to Know Before You Talk to the Right Lawyer

🔑 Key Takeaways

  1. Don't DIY your estate plan. Only an expert knows what's missing — from divorce protection clauses for your children to a trust protector provision. Boilerplate forms and online services leave critical gaps.

  2. Review your estate plan every one to three years. Laws change unpredictably. The SECURE Act dramatically altered how inherited IRAs and 401(k)s are taxed, catching many families off guard.

  3. If your trust was set up before 2012, it likely has an AB Trust provision that is now not only unnecessary but potentially harmful to multigenerational wealth due to 2011 law changes.

  4. In 2026, the federal death tax exemption is set to be cut roughly in half — from ~$12M to ~$6M per person. If your estate exceeds that threshold, specialist planning is urgent.

  5. Choose a certified specialist, not a generalist. Look for state bar certified specialists or members of NAELA, the American Academy of Estate Planning Attorneys, or similar credentialing bodies.

Only an expert knows what's missing. You can read a document and figure out what it says — but there are things that are missing, and that's what gets people in trouble. — James Cunningham

Lawmakers get together, decide something is a good idea, and then we're all left trying to deal with it. One major change between 2017 and now: there was a massive shift in how the government treats inherited retirement accounts — IRAs and 401(k)s. Most people, about 87 percent of the time, take all the money out of that account. And here's the problem: you take money out of an IRA or a 401(k), you're going to pay income tax on it. In California, where I'm based, between federal and state, you're getting close to 50 percent.

[0:04:11] BB: Goodness. Okay, this book is clearly meant to help a very specific group of people. When you think of the ideal reader, where are they in life when they're picking it up?

[0:04:42] James Cunningham: I think this book is for anyone who cares — who cares about their loved ones and what happens to their stuff when they die. At the beginning of the book, we talk about how we can agree or disagree on religion, on politics, but it is a fact: you come into this world with nothing and you leave everything behind. A savvy estate planning attorney is going to ask you a series of questions. This book is designed to get you thinking about those answers before you go.

[0:06:32] BB: Let's define what an estate plan actually is, because people have different ideas. What's your definition?

[0:06:40] James Cunningham: An estate plan is kind of like saying a "garden." What is a garden? It means different things to different people. But fundamentally, an estate plan is a set of documents — instructions to people, institutions, and the government on: who is in charge, who gets what, and when. Part of it is also healthcare decision-making. A lot of people don't think of that as estate planning, but especially during COVID, situations arose where only one person could visit a loved one. Part of estate planning is naming a guardian for minor children, protecting assets from divorce, and deciding who inherits. I mean, that's what most people picture — reading a will on a dark and stormy night at the lawyer's office in front of a fireplace.

[0:08:02] BB: That is exactly what I picture.

[0:08:03] James Cunningham: Bats flying around or something.

[0:08:04] BB: What are the most common errors you see?

[0:08:23] James Cunningham: The number one mistake is people trying to do it on their own — whether they handwrite a will, use an online service, or pay $399 for boilerplate. And this is why you need an expert: only an expert knows what's missing. A layperson can read a legal document and figure out what it says. But there are things that are absent. For example: half of marriages end in divorce. Many people who inherit from their parents end up having that inheritance claimed as marital or community property by a spouse. Most estate planning attorneys do not focus on divorce protection for your children. If that's important to you, you need it specifically written in. Another thing is the trust protector. A trustee has to follow the trust as written — which is usually fine, but sometimes creates problems years later that no one foresaw. The trust protector is an independent party who has the power to modify the trust document in specific, defined ways — without having to go to court. If you don't have one, you're stuck litigating. I recently met with people who ignored the fact that their mother had passed away. Large estate — not only will they owe death taxes, they'll face a two-million-dollar penalty plus interest at $500 a day. The bigger the numbers, the bigger the consequences.

[0:11:46] BB: I always assumed estate planning was for a certain level of wealth. Is there a dollar threshold or age when someone should start thinking about this?

[0:12:12] James Cunningham: I'd describe it as a barbell. On one end: elderly people who simply own their home. The home is their largest asset and often the thing most at risk if they go into assisted living — that runs $10,000 a month. Protecting that home requires a very specific type of estate plan. On the other end: people with over $5 million, or an IRA or 401(k) over $100,000 — they genuinely need a specialist because the tax exposure is enormous. The majority of people are somewhere in the middle, and they need a competent attorney, though perhaps not an uber-specialist.

[0:13:17] BB: What should we look for when selecting an attorney?

[0:13:31] James Cunningham: Many states have a certified specialist program — California is one of them. It requires an additional bar exam, additional education requirements, and a minimum number of estate planning matters handled. For states without that, look for membership in the National Academy of Elder Law Attorneys (NAELA), the American Academy of Estate Planning Attorneys, Wealth Counsel, or the National Network of Estate Planning Attorneys. These organizations have barriers to entry and signify serious practitioners. Above all, pick someone for whom estate planning is a major focus — not someone who practices five different areas of law. And regardless of who you choose, review your estate plan with your attorney every three to five years. Life changes. Laws change. Your estate plan may simply be out of date.

[0:15:08] BB: What are some important things to consider when it comes to the living trust?

[0:15:12] James Cunningham: A living trust — also called a revocable trust — has been around for about a thousand years, since English trust law in the time of the Crusades. It's a trust you can amend or revoke at any time. If you have a living trust created before 2012, you likely have what's called an AB Trust provision. An AB Trust mandates that on the death of one spouse, assets are split — half goes into an irrevocable "B Trust." The reason that existed was for tax purposes, but a 2011 law change eliminated the need for most people. Now, not only is an AB Trust often unnecessary, it can actually harm the creation of multigenerational wealth. That's a red flag to look for when reviewing your documents.

[0:16:36] BB: Are there other major law changes that drove the need for a second edition?

[0:16:50] James Cunningham: The really big one: in 2017, the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act doubled the federal death tax exemption. As of early 2022, you can pass roughly $12 million free of federal estate tax. But here's the critical part — in 2026, that exemption is set to be cut in half. Anything above approximately $6 million (plus inflation) will be taxed at 40 percent. And that bill is due nine months after death. If you have a substantial estate, the window to plan around this is closing.
